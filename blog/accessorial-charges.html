Accessorial Charges Explained: Liftgates, Residential, and More

Updated: January 30, 2026 â€¢ 7 min read

Freight rates are fundamentally calculated based on a "Standard Dock-to-Dock" assumption. This means the carrier expects a 53-foot trailer to pull into a commercial loading dock where a forklift is ready, the driver does not have to help, and the street is wide enough for a tractor-trailer. Anything that deviates from this "perfect" scenarioâ€”any special equipment, special locations, or special handlingâ€”is billed as an Accessorial Charge.

In 2026, accessorials are the primary area where "Cheap" freight quotes turn into "Expensive" freight invoices. If you don't declare these requirements upfront, carriers often apply "Corrected" fees after the delivery, which are frequently 50-100% higher than the pre-booked rate. Understanding these line items is the difference between a profitable shipment and a financial loss.

The "Big 3" Accessorials: The Most Common Surcharges

1. Liftgate Service ($50 - $150)

Standard freight trucks are designed for loading docks (48 to 52 inches high). If the shipper or consignee (the receiver) does not have a loading dock or a forklift, the truck must be equipped with a hydraulic liftgate to lower the pallet from the bed to the ground.
The Gap: Not all trucks have liftgates. If you don't request one and a non-liftgate truck arrives, the driver will "Abort" the delivery. You will then be billed for a "Re-delivery" fee PLUS the original liftgate fee.

2. The "Residential" Trap ($75 - $150)

A common misconception is that "Residential" refers to a home. In 2026 carrier tariffs, a residential delivery is defined as **any location that is not a commercial property with a dock**.
This includes:
â€¢ Home offices in upscale neighborhoods.
â€¢ Farmhouses or agricultural properties.
â€¢ Businesses located on "Dead End" streets or narrow residential lanes.
Carriers use "AI Location Auditing" to cross-reference addresses against zoning data and satellite imagery. If their algorithm sees a driveway instead of a dock, the fee is auto-applied to your invoice.

3. Limited Access ($50 - $100)

This fee is often confused with Residential, but it applies to commercial or government sites that are "Hard to Get To."
Common triggers include:
â€¢ **Schools and Universities:** High pedestrian traffic and security gates.
â€¢ **Construction Sites:** Unpaved roads and dynamic unloading areas.
â€¢ **Hospitals:** Strict receiving hours and "No Idle" zones.
â€¢ **Storage Units:** Narrow corridors where a 53-foot trailer physically cannot turn.

Inside Delivery vs. "White Glove"

An **Inside Delivery** ($75 - $150) fee is triggered the moment the driver takes the pallet through the "First Threshold" of the building. In most standard LTL contracts, "Delivery" means dropping the pallet on the ground (via liftgate) or at the dock. If the driver brings that pallet into the lobby, garage, or elevator, the clock starts on an Inside Delivery charge.

**Warning:** Inside delivery does not mean "White Glove." The driver will not unwrap the pallet, carry items up stairs, or remove debris. If you need that level of service, you are looking for a **Final Mile Specialist**, not a standard LTL carrier.

The "Time is Money" Fees: Detention and Layover

Drivers have strict HOS (Hours of Service) regulations. If they are sitting at your dock, they aren't making money.
**Detention:** Most carriers give 30 minutes of "Free Time." If you take 45 minutes to unload, you will be billed for "Detention" at a rate typically around $50 to $75 per 15-minute increment. In 2026, trucks use GPS and geofencing to auto-log their "Arrival" and "Departure" times, making it impossible to "Negotiate" away a detention fee with the driver.

Sorting and Segregating ($0.75 - $1.50 per piece)

If you ship a pallet containing 50 individual boxes and the receiver asks the driver to "Break the shrink wrap and count/match each box against the packing slip," you will be hit with a **Sort & Seg** fee. This is common at retail distribution centers (like Walmart or Target). It is almost always cheaper to palletize goods by SKU to avoid this manual labor surcharge.

The 2026 "Tech Fee"

A new accessorial appearing in late 2025/2026 is the **Admin Audit Fee** or **Verification Fee**. This is a flat charge ($15 - $35) for the carrier to run your pallet through their Dimensioning machine (Cubiscan) to verify your "Self-Declared" weight and class. Shippers who consistently provide accurate data from tools like ours can often get this "Trust Fee" waived in their annual contracts.

Overlength and Oversized Pallets

LTL trailers use 96-inch to 102-inch widths. If your pallet is 144 inches (12 feet) long, it occupies the space of three standard pallets.
â€¢ **8ft+ Overlength:** ~$150 surcharge.
â€¢ **12ft+ Overlength:** ~$300+ surcharge (often moves to FTL or Volume rates).
Carriers hate overlength because they cannot "Stack" anything on top or beside it, creating "Dead Air" in the trailer that you must pay for.

Conclusion: The Cost of the "Missed" Accessorial

The golden rule of logistics is **Declaration**. If you need a liftgate, declare it. If you are shipping to a school, flag it. The cost of a "Re-delivery" attempt after a driver fails to unload is often more than the original liftgate fee.
