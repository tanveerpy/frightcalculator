Phase 1: Carrier Liability (The "Bailee" Trap)

By law, under the **Carmack Amendment**, carriers acting as a "bailee" are responsible for the goods in their possession. However, this is **Limited Liability**, not "Full Value Protection." It is governed by the carrier's **Tariff**, a legal document you "agree" to the moment you sign the Bill of Lading.

For example, if you ship "Used Machinery," many carriers cap liability at **$0.10 per pound**. Historically, this was to prevent shippers from claiming old, already-broken machines were "destroyed" in transit. In 2026, these archaic limits still stand, often leaving shippers of refurbished tech with payouts that don't even cover the cost of the freight itself.

Phase 2: The "All-Risk" Shipper's Interest (Full Coverage)

**Shipper's Interest Insurance** (or "Cargo Insurance") is a separate policy bought from an insurance company, not the trucking firm. It is the "Gold Standard" for 2026 logistics.

The primary advantage is **Negligence-Free Coverage**. To win a liability claim against a carrier, you must prove they were at fault. If a tornado rips the roof off a trailer (an "Act of God"), the carrier is legally exonerated from the loss. With Shipper's Interest Insurance, if the goods are damaged, **you get paid regardless of the cause**.

  • Claim Speed: Liability claims take 90-120 days. Insurance claims are often settled in 15-30 days.
  • Full Invoice Value: Insurance covers the cost of the goods + freight + 10% (for administrative hassle).
  • No "Per-Pound" Math: If a 1-lb diamond is lost, insurance pays the diamond's value. Liability pays for one pound of gravel.

Phase 3: The "Declared Value" Trap

Many shippers see a box on the BOL titled "Declared Value" and assume that by entering "$10,000," they have "insured" the load. This is a dangerous myth.
**The Reality:** Declaring a value on a BOL usually only "removes the cap" on liability; it doesn't change the fact that you still have to prove the carrier was negligent. Furthermore, carriers often charge **$0.80 per $100 of value** for this, which is significantly more expensive than a dedicated cargo insurance policy (which might cost $0.35 per $100).

Consequential Loss: The Insurance Hardstop

Even with the best "All-Risk" insurance, there is one thing that is almost never covered: **Consequential Loss**.
If your product arrives broken and you lose a $1M contract because you missed the deadline, insurance will pay for the product, but it will NOT pay for the lost contract. This is why "Time-Critical" freight should always be managed with redundant supply chains, not just insurance policies.

Salvage Rights: Who Owns the Junk?

When an insurance company pays you for a "Total Loss," they "buy" the damaged goods from you. This is called **Salvage Rights**. You cannot collect the $10,000 insurance check AND keep the damaged machine for spare parts. If you want to keep the "junk," the insurer will deduct the "Salvage Value" (usually 10-20%) from your payout.
