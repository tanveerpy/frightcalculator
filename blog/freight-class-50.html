Understanding Freight Class 50: The "Clean Freight" Gold Standard

Updated: January 30, 2026 â€¢ 6 min read

In the high-stakes world of LTL (Less-Than-Truckload) shipping, Freight Class 50 is often referred to as the "Holy Grail" of classifications. It represents the pinnacle of shipping efficiency: cargo that is dense, durable, and exceptionally easy for carriers to handle. Because it poses the lowest risk and consumes the least amount of volume relative to its weight, it commands the lowest shipping rates in the NMFC (National Motor Freight Classification) system.

However, achieving a Class 50 rating isn't just about weight; it's about a precise alignment of density, stowability, ease of handling, and liability. In this guide, we'll dive deep into why Class 50 is the gold standard and how your business can optimize its operations to hit this high-density benchmark.

What Exactly is Class 50?

Class 50 is the bottom of the numerical scale for standard freight classes. In the NMFC system, classes range from 50 to 500. The lower the class, the lower the rate. A Class 50 shipment is essentially "clean freight"â€”it's the type of cargo that carriers love to use as "bottom load" because it stabilizes the trailer and maximizes weight capacity without wasting expensive vertical space.

To qualify for Class 50, a commodity must generally hit a density threshold of 50 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) or greater. But density is only one of the four "transportation characteristics" defined by the NMFTA:

  • Density: The primary driver. Class 50 requires extreme density (think solid metal or stone).
  • Stowability: The cargo must be easy to stack and cannot have irregular shapes that prevent other freight from being loaded around it.
  • Handling: It must be easily moved with standard equipment like a forklift or pallet jack without specialized care.
  • Liability: The risk of damage, theft, or spontaneous combustion must be near zero.

Common Commodities that Hit the Class 50 Mark

Not everything can be Class 50. You could pack a box as tight as possible, but if the material itself isn't intrinsically dense, you won't hit the threshold. Here are the staples of the "Clean 50" club:

Industry Qualified Commodities Typical P.C.F.
Construction Concrete blocks, bricks, natural stone tiles, bags of cement. 60+ pcf
Industrial Steel nuts, bolts, washers, iron castings, lead ingots. 100+ pcf
Print/Paper Cases of copier paper, bound catalogs, print-on-demand books. 50-55 pcf
Raw Materials Sand in super sacks, bags of flour (tightly packed), salt. 52+ pcf

The Financial Impact: Why Density Matters

The price difference between Class 50 and even a slightly higher class like Class 70 can be staggering over a fiscal year. Carriers use "Base Rates" that increase significantly with each class jump. This is because higher-class freight (like furniture or electronics) is "light and airy," meaning it fills up a trailer's volume long before it hits its weight limit.

Consider a 2,000 lb shipment moving from Chicago to Atlanta:

  • Class 50 (Bricks): The carrier can stack 20 other pallets on top of this. Rate: $280.
  • Class 100 (Plastic Parts): The carrier likely can't stack anything on this. Rate: $640.
  • Class 250 (Light Fixtures): Extremely fragile and light. Rate: $1,450.

By optimizing your packaging to move from Class 60 to Class 50, you are essentially "buying" trailer space at a discount.

Case Study: The "Air-Gap" Audit

A mid-sized fastener company was shipping steel bolts in boxes on pallets. Their freight was being billed at Class 70. Upon investigation, it was found that their boxes were only 80% full, and the pallets had a 4-inch "lip" of empty space around the boxes.

The Fix: They switched to a slightly smaller, heavy-duty double-walled box that fit the bolts perfectly and aligned flush with the 48x40 pallet edges. This increased their pallet density from 38 pcf to 52 pcf.

The Result: Their freight class dropped from 70 to 50. They saved an average of $112 per pallet across 400 shipments a year, resulting in a $44,800 annual bottom-line increase.

The "Backhaul" Advantage

Carriers actively hunt for Class 50 freight for their "backhauls"â€”the return trip of a truck to its home terminal. Because Class 50 freight adds stability and weight to a truck that might otherwise be empty or too light to handle safely in high winds, you can often negotiate even lower "Spot Rates" for Class 50 cargo in specific lanes.

Common Pitfalls when Claiming Class 50

Don't fall into the trap of assuming you are Class 50 just because your product is heavy. Carriers use sophisticated laser dimensioners (like CargoSpect) to audit every pallet. If your pallet has overhang (boxes sticking out past the wood), the dimensioner will record the widest point. This "phantom volume" will lower your calculated density and could push you into a more expensive class, triggering a $75-$150 re-classification fee.

The "Liftgate" Trap

Even though Class 50 is cheap to ship, it is heavy. If your delivery location doesn't have a loading dock, you will need a liftgate. Most standard liftgates have a weight limit of 2,500 lbs. If your Class 50 pallet exceeds this, it may require a specialized truck or be refused at the point of delivery, leading to redelivery fees that wipe out your shipping savings.

Conclusion: Mastering the Density Game

Freight Class 50 isn't just a number on a Bill of Lading; it's a strategic advantage. By understanding the mathematics of density and ensuring your packaging is as compact as possible, you turn your logistics department from a cost center into a competitive edge.
